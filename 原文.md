## Highlights

•Highly stretchable electronic skins are fabricated with high sensitivity

•Various signals can be detected by the developed electronic skins

•The prestretching process improves the repeatability of the electronic skin

•A machine learning-combined foot posture analysis system is proposed

## Summary

With the rapid development of biomimetic devices and smart wearables, there is increasing potential for electronic skin (E-skin) applications in the fields of space debris retrieval, medical diagnosis, and rehabilitation. Here, we report the stretch/temperature-sensitive (S/T-S) and pressure-sensitive (P-S) E-skins based on a highly stretchable and microporous carbon nanotube/graphene/thermoplastic polyurethane hybrid film. The gauge factors (GFs) of the S/T-S E-skin are 12.19 and 635.03 in the ε ranges of 0%–340% and 340%–480%, respectively. In addition, the prestretching process largely improves its repeatability during the stretching-releasing cycles. The P-S E-skin has a GF value of 1.79 kPa−1 in the pressure ranges of 5–20,005 Pa. Given their excellent performance, the E-skins are integrated into a biomimetic frog tongue for the detection of environmental temperature, telescopic, and tactile signals. Furthermore, a machine learning-combined foot posture analysis system is also proposed with a high recognition accuracy of 96.2%.

## Introduction

In recent years, electronic skins (E-skins) have been widely developed, enabling them to mimic human skin in terms of perception, flexibility, and ductility.[1](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#),[2](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#),[3](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#),[4](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#),[5](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#),[6](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#),[7](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#),[8](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#) However, several challenges remain for them to achieve a high degree of stretchability and to detect multiple signals, which limit their potential applications in the fields of aerospace, intelligent robotics, and smart wearables. Generally, high stretchability is particularly beneficial for applications in aerospace equipment, biomimetics, and soft robotics, such as parachutes,[9](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#),[10](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#) octopus-inspired sensorized soft arms,[11](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#),[12](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#) and chameleon-like soft robots.[13](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#),[14](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#),[15](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#) In addition, the ability to perceive multiple signals is vital for creating smart wearables that can interact seamlessly with their environment and provide comprehensive monitoring in various applications.[16](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#),[17](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#),[18](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#),[19](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#) Addressing these requirements can significantly increase the functionality of E-skins, representing a promising direction for the development of cutting-edge sensor technologies.

Although highly stretchable E-skins have been widely developed, combining their outstanding stretchability with superior sensitivity remains a significant challenge. To date, many researchers have investigated various approaches to address this issue.[20](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#),[21](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#),[22](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#),[23](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#),[24](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#),[25](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#),[26](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#) Zhang et al. employed a vacuum infiltration method to prepare a carbon nanotube (CNT)/graphene oxide (GO) film and transferred it onto prestrained elastic tape for the fabrication of a stretch-sensitive (S-S) E-skin, which achieved a gauge factor (GF) of 287.6 with a relatively small stretching range of 0%–100%.[20](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#) On the basis of the PVA-Gp/TA-CaCl2 hydrogel, Chen et al. developed an S-S E-skin with a stretching range of 0%–370%, but it had a low GF of 1.73, and the hydrogel also tended to dehydrate over time, posing significant challenges for its long-term use due to the loss of mechanical performance.[21](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#) In addition, Amjadi et al. employed an air-spray coating method to fabricate a CNT/Ecoflex hybrid film-based S-S E-skin with a stretching range of 0%–500%, but it still had a low GF of 2.42.[22](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#) According to their results, the E-skin suffered from either a narrow detection range or low sensitivity. To achieve high sensitivity with a large stretchability range, many researchers have used the electrospinning method to prepare thermoplastic polyurethane (TPU) microfiber films, which are combined with various conductive nanomaterials to fabricate S-S E-skins.[27](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#),[28](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#),[29](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#),[30](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#),[31](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#) Generally, electrospinning technology has the ability to produce micrometer-scale ultrafine fibers, which impart excellent stretchability and a porous honeycomb structure that facilitates effective stretching sensing capabilities and offers channels for the penetration of nanomaterials.[32](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#),[33](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#),[34](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#),[35](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#) Wang et al. fabricated a reduced GO (rGO)/TPU composite film via ultrasonic treatment and achieved an S-S E-skin with a stretching range of 0%–250% and a maximum GF of 155.7.[27](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#) Zhou et al. fabricated a CNT/TPU hybrid film via a spray coating method and prepared an S-S E-skin with a stretching strain (ε) of up to 710% and a maximum GF of approximately 40.[28](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#) Ren et al. prepared a CNT/TPU-based E-skin via ultrasonication, and its stretching range was from 0 to 900%, while its maximum GF was 20.[29](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#) Although a large stretching range was easily achieved for conductive nanomaterial/TPU hybrid film-based S-S E-skins, their sensitivity still needs to be further improved, and only a single signal can be perceived, which limits their potential applications.

To solve the above issues, we propose two types of CNT/G/TPU-based E-skins, stretch/temperature-sensitive (S/T-S) and pressure-sensitive (P-S) E-skins, with various signals successfully detected. As mentioned above, both CNTs and graphene (G) are widely used for the fabrication of E-skins because of their high carrier mobility, excellent chemical stability, and good thermal and electrical conductivity.[36](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#),[37](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#),[38](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#) Generally, one-dimensional (1D) CNTs are beneficial to the stretchability of E-skins, whereas two-dimensional (2D) G is beneficial for improving their sensitivity.[39](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#),[40](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#),[41](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#) Thus, both CNTs and G are introduced on the surface of TPU microfibers to fabricate highly stretchable S/T-S E-skins with high sensitivity, and their optimal mixing ratios are explored. Moreover, the sensing performance and mechanisms of the E-skins are elucidated during the detection of ε, temperature, and pressure signals. Finally, the prepared E-skins are successfully applied in the fields of biomimetic devices and smart wearables.

## Results and discussion

### Choice of the hybrid film

As shown in [Figures 1](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#fig1)A–1E, a flexible and stretchable CNT/G/TPU hybrid film with a wrinkled surface was prepared by an ultrasonic dip-coating method, and the S/T-S E-skin was fabricated based on this film. As shown in [Figures 2](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#fig2)A and [S1](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#mmc1), the relationship between the normalized resistance variation (ΔR/R0) and ε of the E-skins with different G contents was analyzed. The sensitivity of the E-skin was judged by the GF, which was defined as (ΔR/R0)/(ΔL/L0), where ΔR is the resistance variation, R0 is the resistance in the unstretched state, ΔL is the length variation, and L0 is the length in the unstretched state. [Figure 2](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#fig2)B shows that when the G content increased, the maximum GF of the E-skin increased, but its maximum ε decreased accordingly. For the CNT/TPU film-based S/T-S E-skin, the CNTs adhered to the surface of the TPU microfiber as elongated filaments in the hybrid film, as shown in [Figure 2](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#fig2)C. Generally, 1D CNTs have a high aspect ratio, which improves the stretchability of the E-skin but inversely deteriorates its sensitivity due to the inconspicuous variation in resistance.[40](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#) Thus, it had the largest detection range of up to 540% but the lowest GF value of 24.78, as shown in [Figure S1](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#mmc1)A. For the G/TPU film-based S/T-S E-skin, G adhered to the surface of the TPU microfiber as lamellar sheets in the hybrid film, as shown in [Figure 2](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#fig2)D. Compared with those of 1D CNTs, the connection pathways of two-dimensional (2D) G are easier to disrupt.[42](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#) Thus, it had the highest GF value of 2133.45 but the smallest detection range of 0%–200%, as shown in [Figure S1](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#mmc1)G. For the CNT/G/TPU film-based S/T-S E-skin, the CNTs ensured the large detection range of the E-skin, and the G guaranteed its high sensitivity. To achieve a large ε (>450%) with a high GF value (>600), a G content of 60 wt % was selected for the fabrication of the S/T-S E-skin

As shown in [Figures 2](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#fig2)E and [S2](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#mmc1)A, S2B, and S2E, the CNT/G/TPU hybrid film inherited the porous microstructure of the original TPU film, and both elongated CNTs and lamellar G sheets were successfully attached to the surface of the TPU microfiber after ultrasonication treatment. Generally, the porous architecture of the TPU film provided pathways that allowed CNTs and G to infiltrate and adhere to the internal surfaces. In addition, the interfacial interaction between the CNTs/G and TPU microfibers was also improved by ultrasonication, and a possible interaction mechanism was also reported.[29](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#),[43](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#),[44](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#) Cavitation bubbles formed in deionized water around the ultrasonic amplitude transformer bar during the sonication process. As the bubbles approached the surface of the CNTs and G, their collapse produced microjets and shock waves with high energy, which pushed the CNTs and G toward the TPU microfiber surface at high speed, resulting in interfacial collisions between them.[45](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#),[46](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#) The collisions softened or partially melted the microfibers at the impact sites, allowing the CNTs and G to anchor onto the microfiber surface.[27](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#) The interfacial chemical interactions between CNTs/G and TPU were analyzed by Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR), as shown in [Figure S3](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#mmc1). After CNT and G incorporation, several characteristic peaks of the TPU showed noticeable shifts. For example, the peak of the C–H band shifted from 2954 cm−1 to 2952 cm−1, the peak of the N–H band shifted from 1529 cm−1 to 1527 cm−1, and the peak of the urethane carbonyl (–H–N–COO–) band shifted from 1727 cm−1 to 1726 cm−1. These variations revealed strong interactions between the TPU macromolecular chains and CNTs/G, which improved the electromechanical properties of the CNT/G/TPU hybrid films.[29](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#) The resistivity of the original TPU film was measured to be 5.46 × 109 Ω·m, but this value was significantly reduced to 2.44 Ω·m for the CNT/G/TPU hybrid film. It was confirmed that CNTs/G adhered to the surface of the TPU microfibers, forming conductive paths, while the CNT/G-decorated conductive microfibers also joined together, thereby constructing a continuous conductive network within the hybrid film. As shown in [Figure S4](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#mmc1), the mechanical properties of the CNT/G/TPU, G/TPU, and CNT/TPU hybrid films were analyzed. The tensile strength and elongation at break of the CNT/G/TPU hybrid film were 22 MPa and 516%, and its Young’s modulus was calculated to be 1.23 MPa. Besides, as the G content increased, the tensile strength and elongation at break of the hybrid films first decreased and then remained basically unchanged. In addition, as shown in [Figure S5](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#mmc1), the hybrid film exhibited excellent thermal stability, with a small weight loss of 0.2% up to 200°C under an N2 environment. In general, the prepared porous conductive CNT/G/TPU hybrid film with a wrinkled surface was also suitable for the fabrication of P-S E-skins. Therefore, a G content of 60 wt % was selected for further study.

### Stretching sensing performance

[Figure 3](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#fig3)A shows a schematic diagram of the sensing mechanism of the S/T-S E-skin during the stretching-releasing process. When the E-skin was stretched, the nonaligned microfibers were elongated and started to be oriented, which reduced the contact area among the CNT/G/TPU conductive microfibers inside the hybrid film.[29](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#),[47](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#) In addition, microcracks bridging the CNTs also formed on the surface of the microfibers, and their gap gradually increased as ε continuously increased. Both of these factors decreased the number of conductive pathways in the film, thereby increasing the overall resistance of the E-skin. As the E-skin was released, the conductive network was rebuilt inside the hybrid film, and its resistance also recovered to the initial state. [Figure 3](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#fig3)B shows the I-V curves of the E-skin with various ε values from 0% to 480%, and all of them were linear, indicating that the E-skin exhibited Ohm’s law with different ε values. As shown in [Figure 3](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#fig3)C, the E-skin maintained a stable state during the stretching process with various ε values varying from 0% to 480%. [Figure 3](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#fig3)D shows that the curve was segmented into two linear parts, 0%–340% (R2 = 0.92) and 340–480% (R2 = 0.98), with extracted GFs of 12.19 and 635.03, respectively. When the ε of the E-skin was varied from 0% to 340%, the nonaligned microfibers were elongated and oriented, as shown in [Figure 3](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#fig3)A. Microcracks also occurred on the surface of the microfibers, and the elongated CNTs connected the separated layers as bridges and partly maintained the conductive network, which increased the detection range of the E-skin but reduced the variation rate of its ΔR/R0 inversely. Therefore, a relatively low GF of 12.19 was achieved in the stretching range of 0%–340%. As the ε of the E-skin was varied from 340% to 480%, the microfibers were further elongated and oriented, and some bonding microfibers also slipped apart. In addition, the microcrack gap also increased, and the bridging function provided by the CNTs, akin to “lotus roots,” gradually diminished. Both of these factors resulted in significant destruction of the conductive network inside the film, leading to a sharp increase in the variation rate of the ΔR/R0 of the E-skin. Therefore, a high GF of 635.03 was achieved in the stretching range of 340%–480%. When ε exceeded 480%, the conductive network was almost damaged, and the E-skin could not work properly. Generally, the response and recovery times refer to the time required for the E-skin to transition its output signal from an initial state (ΔR/R0 = 0) to 90% of the maximum value ((ΔR/R0)max) and from (ΔR/R0)max to 10% of (ΔR/R0)max, respectively.[43](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#) As shown in [Figure 3](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#fig3)E, the response and recovery times of the E-skin were calculated to be 60 and 124 ms at a maximum ε of 10%, after the motor movement time was subtracted. ### Temperature sensing performance

To investigate the temperature sensing performance of the S/T-S E-skin, the RH was maintained at 25% to eliminate the influence of humidity. As shown in [Figure 4](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#fig4)A, when the temperature increased, the surface of the G layers became more wrinkled.[49](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#) This led to an increase in the contact area among the G layers and between the G and CNTs, which increased the total number of conductive pathways inside the CNT/G/TPU hybrid film. Simultaneously, the electron mobility of CNTs and G increased with increasing temperature, leading to an increase in the electrical conductivity of the hybrid film.[43](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#) As a consequence, the E-skin current increased accordingly. During the cooling process, the surface of the G layers was restored, and the electron mobility of the CNTs and G also recovered, which resulted in the recovery of the E-skin current. [Figure 4](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#fig4)B shows that the I-V curves of the E-skin had good linearity in the temperature range of 25°C–85°C, indicating that it obeyed Ohm’s law at different temperatures. The sensitivity of the E-skin to temperature was judged by its GF, which was defined as (ΔI/I0)/ΔT, where ΔI is the current variation, I0 is the current at 25°C, and ΔT is the temperature variation. As shown in [Figure 4](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#fig4)C, the relationship between ΔI/I0 and temperature was linear, and the GF value was calculated to be 3.66 × 10−3°C−1 (R2 = 0.99). The response and recovery times refer to the time required for the E-skin to transition its output signal from an initial state (ΔI/I0 = 0) to 90% of the maximum value ((ΔI/I0)max) and from (ΔI/I0)max to 10% of (ΔI/I0)max, respectively. As shown in [Figure 4](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#fig4)D, the response and recovery times were calculated to be 46 and 58 s, with the temperature varying from 25°C to 85°C. [Figure 4](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#fig4)E shows its hysteresis curve, and the values of ΔHmax and YFS were 2.1 and 19.4, respectively. ### Pressure sensing performance

For the detection of pressure signals, a P-S E-skin was developed on the basis of IDEs and a CNT/G/TPU hybrid film with a G content of 60 wt %, and its sensing mechanism was also analyzed. As shown in [Figure 5](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#fig5)A, the hybrid film had a wrinkled surface and porous microstructure, as confirmed by [Figures 1](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#fig1)B and [S2](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#mmc1)E. During the pressure loading period, the contact area rose between the hybrid film and IDEs and among the CNT/G/TPU microfibers inside the hybrid film. As a result, the total number of conductive pathways increased inside the hybrid film and at its interface, which reduced the E-skin resistance and increased its current. During the unloading period, the contact area was recovered inside the film and at its interface, and the E-skin current was subsequently restored to the initial value. [Figure 5](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#fig5)B shows that the I-V curves of the E-skin had good linearity in the pressure range of 5–50,005 Pa, indicating that it obeyed Ohm’s law at different pressures. The sensitivity of the E-skin to pressure was also judged by its GF, which was defined as (ΔI/I0)/ΔP, where ΔI is the current variation, I0 is the current under no pressure loading, and ΔP is the pressure variation. [Figure 5](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#fig5)C shows that the curve was divided into two linear parts, 5–20,005 Pa (R2 = 0.99) and 20,005–50,005 Pa (R2 = 0.98), with extracted GFs of 1.79 and 0.2 kPa−1, respectively. When the pressure rose from 5 to 20,005 Pa, ΔI/I0 was dominated by the relatively large variation in the contact area between the hybrid film and IDEs, which resulted in a high GF value of 1.79 kPa−1. Nevertheless, as the pressure increased from 20,005 to 50,005 Pa, the variation in contact area was not significant between the film and IDEs, and ΔI/I0 was dominated by the relatively small variation in contact area among the CNT/G/TPU microfibers, which led to a relatively lower value of 0.2 kPa−1. Similar to [Figure 3](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#fig3)E, the response and recovery times were calculated to be 160 and 158 ms, with the pressure varying from 5 to 50,005 Pa, as shown in [Figure 5](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#fig5)D. [Figure 5](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#fig5)E shows the ΔI/I0 of the E-skin during 5 loading-unloading cycles, and the pressure was varied from 5 to 105, 1,505, 10,005, 15,005, 20,005, and 50,005 Pa. The ΔI/I0 signals of the E-skin were consistent under the same loading pressure, which suggested excellent cycling stability. [Figure 5](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#fig5)F shows the results of its long-term durability test, and the ΔI/I0 was measured at pressures varying from 5 to 50,005 Pa during 3,000 loading-unloading cycles. ### Applications of the E-skins
As previously mentioned, the S/T-S and P-S E-skins were developed on the basis of the use of a CNT/G/TPU hybrid film as a sensing layer, and various signals, such as ε, temperature, and pressure, were successfully detected. Therefore, these E-skins could be applied to bionic robots and precisely emulate the functionality of biological skin. Here, we designed and fabricated a biomimetic device that replicated the predatory behavior and signal perception of a frog tongue, as shown in [Figure 6](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#fig6)A. This device integrated the S/T-S and P-S E-skins onto an actuator rod. The P-S E-skin at the tip of the biomimetic frog tongue collected tactile signals upon predating prey, while the actions of extending the tongue to hunt and the temperature variations in the surrounding environment were fed back through the S/T-S E-skin on the epidermis of the tongue. [Figure 6](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#fig6)B illustrates the initial, contact, capture, and retrieval stages of the biomimetic tongue during the predatory process. Correspondingly, the electrical signal fluctuations of the P-S E-skin and S/T-S E-skin after the prestretching process were also detected during the predatory process, and the signals of tongue elongation and prey predation were successfully monitored in real time. In the initial stage, the ΔI/I0 value of the P-S E-skin and the ΔR/R0 value of the S/T-S E-skin were 0 and the initial value of 7 after the prestretching process. The E-skins had no pressure loading or ε, indicating that the tongue neither contacted an object nor elongated. In the contact stage, an increment of ΔR/R0 indicated the extension of the tongue, and as a sudden increase in ΔI/I0 was detected, the front end of the tongue touched an object. In the capture stage, ΔI/I0 reached the maximum value and remained constant, indicating that the tongue captured the object. Under this condition, the extension of the tongue was stopped; thus, ΔR/R0 also reached the maximum value and remained constant. In the retrieval stage, a decrease in ΔR/R0 indicated contraction of the tongue, and ΔI/I0 remained constant, confirming that the object had been captured steadily. [Figure 6](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#fig6)C shows infrared images of the tongue epidermis with different surface temperatures. A thermal radiation lamp was used to irradiate the tongue surface, simulating the temperature variations in the surrounding environment of a frog. The S/T-S E-skin on the tongue surface successfully detected temperature variations in the surrounding environment, thereby determining if the conditions were suitable for hunting. Initially, the tongue was placed at room temperature, and the ΔI/I0 of the S/T-S E-skin was 0. When the lamp was turned on, the surface temperature of the tongue increased, which led to an increase in ΔI/I0. After the target temperature was reached, the lamp was turned off, and the surface temperature was reduced accordingly, which led to a decrease in ΔI/I0 and its recovery. Furthermore, various surface temperatures resulted in different values of ΔI/I0, which confirmed that the tongue had the ability to perceive environmental temperatures. Because of its ability to perceive multiple signals, the biomimetic frog tongue has many potential applications, especially in the field of space debris retrieval, as shown in [Figure 6](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#fig6)A.

As shown in [Figure 7](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#fig7)A, one notable application of our prepared E-skins lies in the field of smart wearables, which can be used to monitor the health and recovery status of athletes. In general, the stretching strain of human skin is up to 75%, which allows free movement of the joints.[50](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#) The E-skins were integrated into an intelligent kneepad and smart insole, facilitating real-time monitoring of knee joint motion and gait signals of athletes, as well as the temperature of thermotherapy for health assessment, disease diagnosis, and the evaluation of sports rehabilitation training. The obtained data were remotely transmitted to a terminal via the internet, and telediagnosis could be achieved for athletes. As depicted in [Figure 7](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#fig7)B, both the intelligent kneepad and smart insole comprised four main components: (1) S/T-S and P-S E-skins integrated into the intelligent kneepad and smart insole as the output channels, respectively; (2) a voltage divider circuit and a DC power supply providing a voltage of 3 V; (3) an NI acquisition card for signal collection and data visualization on a terminal via a LabVIEW program; and (4) a cloud drive for data transmission to a remote terminal and a LabVIEW program for signal display. As shown in [Figure 7](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#fig7)C, the S/T-S E-skin was integrated into the key locomotion point at the inner side of the intelligent kneepad, and the degrees of knee flexion, as well as the temperature of thermotherapy were tracked in real time. When the degree of knee flexion increased, the total number of conductive pathways within the CNT/G/TPU hybrid film decreased, which led to an increase in the E-skin resistance. As the degree of knee flexion returned to the initial value, the quantity of conductive pathways and E-skin resistance also recovered. In addition, during thermotherapy with the intelligent kneepad, the total number of conductive pathways inside the hybrid film increased, and the electron mobility of the CNTs and G also increased with increasing temperature, which led to an increase in the E-skin current. According to the ΔI/I0 values of the E-skin, the temperatures were monitored in real time and closely matched those obtained by the infrared thermal imager, which confirmed the detection accuracy of the E-skin. As shown in [Figure 7](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#fig7)D, the P-S E-skins were embedded into key compression points of the smart insole, and various foot postures were tracked in real time. The surface pressure distribution was acquired on the basis of the ΔI/I0 distribution of E-skins, and the smart insole successfully distinguished different foot postures, including normal contact, forefoot contact, heel contact, medial tilt, and lateral tilt. To improve the efficiency and accuracy of foot posture judgment, we employed a K-nearest neighbor (KNN) algorithm to analyze the surface pressure data of different foot postures and make judgments. As shown in [Figure 7](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#fig7)E, the data processing was composed of two parts: training and testing. The dataset of 1,000 foot posture samples was collected and randomly split into two groups, with 80% of the samples used for training and 20% for testing. [Figure 7](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#fig7)F shows the confusion matrix of the model classification result, and our proposed machine learning-combined foot posture analysis system achieved a high recognition accuracy of 96.2% on 200 testing datasets. The results demonstrated the effectiveness of the system in judging foot posture correctness, which could greatly improve the development of gait monitoring technology in the field of medical diagnosis and rehabilitation, especially for injured athletes.

As shown in [Table 1](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#tbl1), the key performance parameters of recently reported stretchable piezoresistive E-skins were summarized, including sensitivity (GF), detection range, and reliability (Dz/Da). Compared with them, our S/T-S E-skin achieved a high sensitivity of 635.03 with a wide detection range of 0%–480% simultaneously and maintained a higher reliability with the Dz and Da of 3.5% and 0.7%, respectively. Furthermore, compared with most previously reported E-skins, more types of signals were detected by the prepared E-skins, including ε, temperature, and pressure. These advantages were beneficial for the applications of the E-skins in the fields of biomimetic devices and smart wearables.

In this work, S/T-S and P-S E-skins were fabricated on the basis of a highly stretchable and microporous CNT/G/TPU hybrid film, and various signals, including ε, temperature, and pressure, were successfully perceived. The S/T-S E-skin had GF values of 12.19 and 635.03 in the stretching ranges of 0%–340% and 340%–480% and 3.66 × 10−3°C −1 in the temperature range of 25°C–85°C. The P-S E-skin had GF values of 1.79 and 0.2 kPa−1 in the pressure ranges of 5–20,005 Pa and 20,005–50,005 Pa, respectively. Besides, the prestretching process could largely improve the repeatability of the E-skin and keep its maximum ΔR/R0 value almost constant for a fixed ε during the stretching-releasing cycles, which solved the common instability issue of E-skins under large ε values and increased the feasibility of its application. Furthermore, the S/T-S and P-S E-skins were integrated into a biomimetic frog tongue, whose extension, environmental temperature, and tactile signals were successfully monitored. In addition, the S/T-S and P-S E-skins were integrated into an intelligent kneepad and smart insole, respectively. The knee joint motion and temperature of thermotherapy were monitored, and a machine learning-combined foot posture analysis system was also proposed, which achieved a high recognition accuracy of 96.2%.

## Methods

### Materials and chemicals

The TPU particles (1185A) were acquired from BASF Co., Ltd., and Ecoflex (00-30) was purchased from Smooth-On, Inc. The MWCNTs (TNSM0) and G (XF001W) were purchased from Chengdu Organic Chemicals Co., Ltd. and Jiangsu Xianfeng Nanomaterial Technology Co., Ltd., respectively. N,N-Dimethylformamide (DMF; 68-12-2) and sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS; 151-21-3) were purchased from Shanghai Aladdin Biochemical Technology Co., Ltd. Polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS, Sylgard 184), polyethylene terephthalate (PET), and nanosilver paste (4929N) were purchased from Dow Corning Co., Ltd., Yingkou Opiveit Material Technology Co., Ltd., and DuPont Chemical, respectively. Chromium (Cr; 99.9%), gold (Au; 99.999%), and aluminum (Al; 99.999%) were purchased from Zhongnuo Advanced Material (Beijing) Technology Co., Ltd.

### Fabrication of the hybrid film

First, 5 g of TPU particles were weighed and mixed with 20 mL of DMF solution in a beaker. The mixture was stirred at 50°C for 4 h to completely dissolve the TPU particles by using a magnetic stirrer. Once cooled to room temperature, the solution was transferred into a 10 mL syringe for electrospinning. The process parameters were set as follows: humidity of 42%, applied voltage of 15 kV, spinning distance of 15 cm, rotor collection device rotation speed of 200 rpm, syringe supply rate of 2 mL/h, and transverse movement speed of the syringe of 60 cm/min. After 4 h of electrospinning, a TPU film with an average thickness of approximately 0.2 mm was obtained, and then it was cut into long strips of 10 mm × 40 mm. Second, 2.24 mg of CNTs and 3.36 mg of G were weighed and placed into a solution with 40 mL of deionized water and 0.05 g of SDS as a foaming agent. After that, the solution was subjected to ultrasonic treatment for 40 min using an ultrasonic cell disintegrator with a power of 650 W and a power ratio of 50%. An alternating-cycle ultrasonic mode was adopted, with each cycle consisting of 5 s of ultrasonication followed by a 5-s pause. This process was carried out to avoid excessive temperature rise during the ultrasonic process, which could cause the CNTs to agglomerate. Through this procedure, we successfully obtained an aqueous dispersion with a concentration of 0.014 wt %. The TPU film was subsequently placed into the aqueous solution and subjected to ultrasonic treatment for 40 min, followed by thorough rinsing with deionized water to remove any loosely adhered CNTs and G on the surface of the film. Afterward, the film was transferred to an oven at 80°C for 1 h, and the final CNT/G/TPU film was obtained, as depicted in [Figure 1](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#fig1)A.

### Fabrication of the E-skins

For the S/T-S E-skin, Al electrodes (100-nm thick) were deposited on both ends of the CNT/G/TPU hybrid film (1 cm × 4 cm) via shadow-masked thermal evaporation, and copper foil was attached to the electrodes through silver paste. After that, the film was packaged with Ecoflex to fabricate the E-skin, as shown in [Figure 1](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#fig1)E. For the P-S E-skin, Cr/Au interdigitated electrodes (IDEs; thickness of 10 nm/50 nm) were first deposited on a PET substrate by shadow-masked thermal evaporation, and copper foils were connected to the IDEs using silver paste. After that, the hybrid film was cut into 10 mm × 10 mm pieces and placed on the surface of the PET substrate with IDEs. Finally, a layer of PDMS was applied to the sample for encapsulation of the E-skin, as shown in [Figure 1](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-physical-science/fulltext/S2666-3864\(26\)00015-9#fig1)F.

### Characterization

The morphology of the original TPU and CNT/G/TPU hybrid films was observed by using field-emission scanning electron microscopy (FE-SEM; JSM-7500F, JEOL) and digital microscopy (ZY-HD2K, Zongyuan Weiye Technology Co., Ltd.). The interfacial chemical interactions between CNTs/G and TPU were analyzed by an FTIR (Nicolet AVATAR380, Thermo Nicolet). The thermal stability of the CNT/G/TPU hybrid film was measured by a thermogravimetric analyzer (TGA; TGA550, TA Instruments). The mechanical properties of the CNT/G/TPU, G/TPU, and CNT/TPU hybrid films were measured by an electronic universal testing machine (Instron 5943). The resistivity of the films was measured by a four-point probe (TSP-2000-RES, Tektronik). For the stretching test of the S/T-S E-skin, the sample was extended with a programmable motor-controlled stepper (Beijing Beiguang Century Instrument Co. Ltd.). For the temperature test of the S/T-S E-skin, a constant temperature and humidity test chamber (DHTHM-16-0-P-SD, DHT) was used, and the relative humidity (RH) was set at 25%. For the pressure test of the P-S E-skin, a _z_ axis precision displacement lifting platform (Beijing Beiguang Century Instrument Co. Ltd.) was employed alongside a high-precision pressure sensor (ULC-2 N, Interface) with a minimum detection accuracy of 0.001 N and a wide-range pressure sensor (MARK-10) with a detection range of up to 100 N. A Keithley 2635B digital source meter was used as the data test module, which could quickly and accurately measure the resistance or current of the E-skins. For the intelligent kneepad and smart insole, the electrical signals were collected by an NI acquisition card (9205, National Instruments).
